Cross-Modal
Brain MRI Retrieval

Our challenge: given a T1 post-contrast MRI, find the T2 of the same patient in a gallery of candidates. Scored by macro-averaged MRR across three datasets, each worth exactly one third. The story I want to tell is not about the final number — it's about what we learned by asking the right questions at each step.
The Task

Match each T1 query → its T2 in the gallery

Score = mean MRR across three datasets · two of three have no labels

Dataset Geometry Labels What breaks
dataset1 Shared grid 350 pairs Nothing — easy
dataset2 Query & target deformed independently None Geometry
dataset3 Target resampled into query's space None Content (surgery)
dataset3 looks hardest (surgery) — but dataset2 is the real challenge: it destroys voxel correspondence, so any alignment-based method collapses there.
MRR = 1/rank of the true target. Each dataset is worth exactly one third regardless of size. Two of three datasets have no training labels — two-thirds of the score is a pure generalization test. This constraint shaped our entire method choice.
Baseline

The obvious move — deep embeddings — fails

DINOv2 ViT-B/14 · 3 axial slices · cosine similarity

~0.20 macro MRR — barely above random
  • Global pooling discards where structures are — the thing that identifies a patient
  • Domain gap — trained on natural photos, not grayscale MRI
  • No modality bridge — nothing crosses the T1↔T2 contrast gap
We also tried a fine-tuned variant with per-modality projection heads trained on the 350 labeled dataset1 pairs. Same result — the architecture doesn't help when global pooling discards the signal you need. This told us the problem wasn't solvable by representation learning alone.
Reframe

An alignment problem,
not a representation problem.

The identifying signal lives in whether voxels correspond — exactly what global embeddings throw away.

Wrong question

"Do these images look similar?"

Right question

"Do the same voxels
carry the same anatomy?"

T1 and T2 of the same brain look completely different pixel-by-pixel — fluid is dark in T1, bright in T2. You cannot match them by appearance or raw correlation. But if they are spatially aligned, every voxel samples the same anatomical location. That spatial relationship is what defines "same patient" — and it's what we need to measure.
Method

Mutual Information — no training, no labels

Joint histogram (left) + per-slice NMI: matched pair (blue) stays above the impostor (red).

NMI = ( H(q) + H(t) ) / H(q, t)
  • Same patient, aligned → T1 voxel predicts T2 voxel → high NMI
  • Wrong patient → anatomy misaligns → relationship washes out → low NMI
  • Measures statistical dependence, not brightness — works across modalities.
We restrict to the brain mask, downsample to ~64³ voxels, and use 32–64 histogram bins. The normalized form is less sensitive to overlap amount than raw MI. No fine-tuning. No dataset-specific training. Pure signal theory — the standard cross-modal registration metric for decades.

Dataset 2 is the entire gap.

These are real Kaggle per-dataset numbers. Submitting results for a single dataset gives score = dataset_MRR / 3. Multiplying by 3 recovers the real per-dataset MRR — a diagnostic trick that let us pinpoint the problem without wasting submissions. Consistent: (0.98 + 0.18 + 0.98) / 3 = 0.713. ✓

Ablation

Deformable registration was worse

ANTs SyN — dense warp 0.846 2.3× the compute
vs
Affine — 12 DoF (kept) 0.884 ds2 validation MRR
Why: a dense warp can deform a wrong patient's brain onto the query, raising impostor similarity and shrinking the gap to the true match. Affine removes pose and scale without enough freedom to fake a match.

For discriminative ranking, the best registration flexibility is lower than for registration quality.

This is a genuine scientific finding, not just an empirical tuning detail. The mechanistic explanation — affine removes nuisance without enabling fake matches — is inferred, not proven. But it's a principled reason that matches the result. We kept affine based on this reasoning, not just luck.
Benchmark Finding

dataset3 leaks the answer
through the file header

  • Three teams scored exactly 1.00000 — content-based methods don't do that
  • Header probe: each target's affine matrix is bit-identical to its matching query's (distance ~10⁻⁹)
  • Perfect bijection: 20/20 val · 77/77 test
  • Cause: targets were resampled into the query's coordinate space, copying that subject's unique per-acquisition geometry
1.00 ds3 via header matching
zero image content used
Content-based (MI): 0.98
both results reported
Reported to the organizers as a data-quality finding — not a shortcut we hide.
Dataset1 and dataset2 have globally constant headers — no leak there. The README actually describes the construction method; the leak is an unintended side effect. Reporting it transparently is the right call. It helps the research community build better benchmarks.

One gap remains: ds2 at 0.88. Top teams at 1.00000 must have a content-based ds2 method stronger than affine+MI — that's the one real remaining research question.
